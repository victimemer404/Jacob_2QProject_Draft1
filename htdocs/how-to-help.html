Help During this Pandemic

    • SUMMARY: Many people are affected by the pandemic since they have lost their jobs either because their industries have shut down or they require less people to operate during this pandemic. They are forced to work other jobs to help themselves. There are people to improve the lives of these people who are most vulnerable during this time.

    However, not all problems are purely financial. Some people are stuck in this quarantine not knowing what to do especially since they are having a hard time during this pandemic. Some are occassionally scared, and some are occassionaly sad or unmotivated. No matter what your problem is, we will help you no matter what in this situation. This article would discuss ways in which we can help people in our own little ways.

    Jump to: Emotional Help | Material Help

    Emotional Help (Self-Care, etc.)

    The COVID-19 pandemic can feel overpowering because of new data, long work hours, and thinking about your family and yourself. It's important to briefly stop for a moment and gather your thoughts, as overall, global pandemics can be burdening. Trying to avoid panicking can help.

    It is entirely expected to feel pushed or overpowered during questionable occasions. Feelings in light of vulnerability may include uneasiness, dread, anger and pity. You likewise could feel powerless, debilitate and, incidentally, wild. Physical reactions may incorporate migraine, muscle pressure, weariness and restlessness.

    According to Mayo Clinic, personal experiences, and other verified reliable sources, here are some ways to avoid the feeling of burn-out and other negative issues during this pandemic.

    Physical Health

    • Eat a healthy, well-balanced diet and drink a lot of water. This way, you get to fuel your body properly by having the right nutrients and materials.
    • Get enough sleep. Try to get seven to nine hours of sleep each night in order to feel-rested in the morning. Along with this, try to keep your sleep schedule as consistent as possible.
    • Exercise, take depe breaths, and stretch often.
    • Avoid risky or destructive behaviors, such as abusing alcohol or drugs, excessive gambling or ignoring public health recommendations. Remember to follow social distancing and wear a mask when you go outside!
    • Spend time outside such as walking around, but make sure to again, follow public safety health recommendations such as social distancing and other guidelines!

    Mental Health

    • Maintain a schedule that you yoruself can keep. This includes things such as work, leisure, and others.
    • Focus on things that are within your control.
    • Use technology such as calling, texting, instant messaging platforms, and video conferencing platforms (Zoom, Google Meet, etc.) in order to maintain social connections with your loved ones. Consider a regular check-in schedule to look forward to something every single day.
    • Focus on the present and write the things that you are grateful of in the present.
    • Listen to music, positive podcasts, read books, and others that you personnally like. Make sure to read books that will nurture your self-growth instead of instilling fear in you.
    • Consume reliable news sources that report facts, and avoid media that sensationalizes emotions. Limit your exposure or take a break from news and social media if you find that it makes you anxious.
    • Lean on personal beliefs (faith, religion, etc.) for support.
    • Look for ways to help your community (donation drives to your favorite organizations, online tutoring services, blood donation drives, checking on the people in the home of the elderly). This will make you appreciate and acknowledge the help that others provide to the community.

    Checking in on Others

    1. Ask if they want to talk about it. It might be that they don’t want to and that’s okay too. Maybe you’re not the right person for them to talk to, but you can make some suggestions.
    2. Listen. Silence may seem awkward at first but think of it as a chance for both of you to gather your thoughts. If you’re finding it difficult to understand what they’re talking about, it’s okay to ask them to explain further.
    3. Support is the most important thing you can offer and if they refuse, help them explore their options for how they could begin to feel better.
